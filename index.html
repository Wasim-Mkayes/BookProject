Chapter 1 : Vulnerability

It’s only when we embrace our vulnerability that we’re able to reach our potential.

Weight loss is much more than counting carbs and protein. Diets provide us with a framework to achieve a calorie deficit that results in weight loss. These tools achieve short-term goals. However, what then? At the core of a sustainable lifestyle change is vulnerability. This is how you defeat the temptation of using food and/or alcohol to help numb, and in many cases, bury the emotions driving your behavior. These bad choices allow you to avoid the messy, unresolved emotional issues in your life. Vulnerability is the key to facing and taking control of your reality

Healing can only begin when you allow yourself to be vulnerable, thereby shutting out the external noise and being authentic. This requires deep emotional work that the majority of people will go out of their way to avoid because it’s uncomfortable, challenging and requires brutal honesty with yourself. However, this is the way to change your eating behavior and ensure sustained success. As Dr. Brene Brown says:

You can’t get to courage without first walking through vulnerability; vulnerability is not about winning or losing, it’s about showing up to be seen.

In such cases, “showing up to be seen” refers to talking about your depression or unhappiness, instead of using sugar to push your feelings away. In doing the latter, you limit access to joy, gratitude and happiness. You have to break the unhealthy cycle.

What Does Being Vulnerable Mean?

Vulnerability is our most accurate measure of courage.

Vulnerability is a state of authenticity that exposes you to the extremes of possible emotional harm and heightened levels of love, empathy, acceptance and connection. Simply put, it is the core of all emotions. Vulnerability empowers you to be honest and true to yourself.

When you share your deepest thoughts, feelings and insecurities, empathy and validation many times fail to greet you. Potential disapproval or anger may face you instead. This is where the fear of other’s judgement comes in as an additional barrier.

You might also meet individuals who view vulnerability as a sign of weakness or a threat to their own safety or comfort. Therefore, they might avoid engaging at all costs.

The combination of this fear and false belief makes it all the harder to embrace vulnerability and grow emotionally. Therefore, you need to recognize it as an asset that helps you reach a greater level of compassion and understanding of yourself and others. It is a foundation to long-term healing and happiness.

It’s so easy to turn to excuses or vices like sugar, alcohol or tobacco to justify our current behavior and completely sidestep vulnerability. When you flee from vulnerability to seek out the status quo and shield yourself, you end up living life trying to blend in. This is nothing more than a defense mechanism, a conflict with who you are at your core. This internal struggle increases your likelihood of turning to any form of addiction, such as unhealthy foods and sugar.

You are capable of tapping into your vulnerability, showing others the level of courage you possess. Vulnerability is key to unlocking deep-seated emotions and resolving internal conflicts that are driving your current mindset and behavior.


The Benefits of Vulnerability in Everyday Life

1. Increases Opportunities for Love and Connection

As you become more vulnerable, the social mask that has helped you fake your way through life becomes less necessary. With this freedom comes an abundance of energy that can be applied to embracing who you are, emotionally and psychologically. As others understand your challenges, this vulnerability opens you up to empathy, understanding, and acceptance. Vulnerability, at its core, is about being human, acknowledging we are not perfect as a means to connect on a deeper level with others.

2. Provides the Strength to Face Challenges

From our greatest weaknesses emerge our greatest strengths. Oftentimes, when you’re going through challenging circumstances, how you react and grow determines your future success. Vulnerability is essential to engaging this process effectively. Recognizing your weaknesses in many ways strengthens your ability to face your obstacles. The missing ingredient many times is letting others in.

3. Creates Authenticity, Which Nurtures Relationships

Dependence on food and/or alcohol lets you retreat from vulnerability in order to suppress bad feelings and experience the short-term pleasure of a dopamine release. This cycle repeats, leading to more shame, guilt, and disappointment. The irony lies in Dr. Brown’s cogent insight: “The things we do to avoid vulnerability end up causing us more pain than vulnerability itself.” However, when you let yourself be vulnerable, our self-worth is constructed on the right building blocks: authenticity, positivity, and hope. With this foundation, your genuine self will find acceptance and forgiveness from those around you. Your connections will deepen as people emotionally connect with the “real” you.

4. Encourages Others Who Identify with Your Struggle

Vulnerability is contagious. When you open up to others, it empowers them to tell their own story while also enabling you to tell yours. Neither of you is alone. Better understanding leads to better identification of each other’s struggles within your corresponding narratives. This leads to speaking up more consistently about struggles and finding camaraderie in this struggle. You are not alone. The power of community is very real.

Roadblocks to Vulnerability

Have the courage to be imperfect, tell the story of who you are with your whole heart. Be willing to let go of who you think you should be in order to be who you are. Vulnerability is the core of shame and fear and our struggle for worthiness, but it appears that it’s also the birthplace of joy, of creativity, of belonging, of love. What makes you vulnerable makes you beautiful.
Dr. Brene Brown

1. Fear of What Others Will Say or Do

Outside influences are constantly driving our behavior. It is much easier to be a follower and simply blend in. To be authentic and true to yourself requires the courage to be vulnerable and commit to a lifestyle that allows you to be the best version of yourself.

2. Denial or Lack of Awareness

You keep pouring energy into convincing yourself that your excess weight doesn’t affect anything beyond the scale. In reality, your unhealthy lifestyle not only erodes all aspects of your life; the denial of this fact lets you avoid vulnerability, the key to starting the healing process.

3. Feelings of Unworthiness or Shame

When we start to see our struggles, weaknesses, and hang-ups, the natural inclination is to hide behind a mask. Our insecurities are exasperated by the expectation of perfection. This unrealistic view does nothing but arise feelings of unworthiness. Realize that ultimately, you are worthy of love, acceptance and connection; flaws are part of this package.


Two Key Steps to Being Vulnerable

Develop Awareness

Awareness has the power to help create deeper and more meaningful connections in your life. By embracing the temporary fear that might come with a better understanding of yourself and the consequence of your actions, you make possible a new level of joy and happiness. The key is letting awareness help you view your behavior for what it is. Then, vulnerability is the next logical step.

Check Your Mindset

Awareness will help you engage your surroundings on a new level. However, your belief and thoughts must drive the change needed to embrace our vulnerability. This mindset is an integral part of your new life: seek it. This is the next step.

Vulnerability provides you with a critical perspective to address the limiting beliefs and emotions driving your current eating behaviors. With it, you are primed to embrace a lifestyle that better reflects the real you.
